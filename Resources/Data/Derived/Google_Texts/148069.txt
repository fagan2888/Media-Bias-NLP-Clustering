Recommended reading from around the Web:

With Western and Iranian negotiators meeting again in Geneva to seek a deal curbing Iran’s nuclear program, Lee Smith (@stronghorsebook) writes that the focus on diplomacy has essentially taken out of the debate the option of using military force, a possibility crucial to keeping pressure on the Iranians: “Talk about bombing Iranian nuclear facilities is now outside the bounds of civilized discourse.” [Weekly Standard]

Jane Harman and Volker Perthes (@volkerperthes), leaders of an American and a German think tank, respectively, write that legislators ought to step in to heal some of the wounds inflicted by the disclosure the NSA was monitoring the cellphone of German Chancellor Angela Merkel; they suggest lawmakers in both countries press for more access to intelligence programs and meet regularly with one another to discuss them. [Reuters]

Investors piled into privatized toll roads, a trendy policy idea in vogue a decade or so ago, but the recession and reduced driving have suppressed traffic and left those investors facing disappointing results, and even bankruptcy in some cases. [WSJ]

The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget counters Larry Summers’ argument that it’s time to stop fretting about the deficit: “No plausible rate of economic growth can reverse growing debt levels,” it says. Acting now allows “smaller and more gradual changes spread over more generations.” [CRFB]

Harold Meyerson (@HaroldMeyerson) says Democrats are no longer divided on social issues or foreign policy but over economics: the Wall Street liberalism of new New Jersey Sen. Cory Booker and Hillary Clinton vs. the anti-corporate populism of new New York City Mayor Bill de Blasio and Sen. Elizabeth Warren. [American Prospect]

Ivor Tossell (@ivortossell) tries to figure out how erratic Rob Ford was elected Toronto’s mayor in the first place. His answer: After seven years of progressive rule in Toronto that led to higher taxes and union unrest, Ford appealed both to angry suburban voters suspicious of downtown interests and poor voters who felt marginalized. [National Journal]

Nancy Hass describes how Stanford economist Caroline Hoxby devised a successful experiment to get high-potential, low-income kids to apply to more selective colleges – at a cost of $6 apiece. [Smithsonian]

Growing economic inequality corrodes social cooperation and willingness to compromise and is breeding discord and political instability in the U.S., Peter Turchin (@Peter_Turchin) of the University of Connecticut writes. He predicts it’s going to get worse. [Bloomberg]

Sign of the Times

Minor milestones we’ve spotted:

China now emits more CO2 per capita than the European Union. [ Washington Post]

In a University of Chicago Booth School survey of big-name academic economists, 89% say that absent any inside information an investor can expect to do better with a low-cost index mutual fund than by picking a few stocks. [Booth]

One quarter of Americans have allergic reactions to cats, 10% to dogs. [WSJ]

At least 3,278 people in the U.S. are serving sentences of life without parole for non-violent crimes, the ACLU says. More than 80% of such sentences were mandatory; the law gave the judge no discretion. [Economist]

U.S. life expectancy rose by almost eight years between 1970 and 2011 to 78.7 years, but grew faster in other countries. The U.S. now lags the OECD average of 80.1. [OECD]

Labels matter. Gallup poll respondents give thumbs down to “Obamacare”: 38% approve while 54% disapprove. But the “Affordable Care Act” does better: 45% approve and 49% disapprove. [Gallup]

German-made cars hold more than 70% of China’s roughly $40 billion annual luxury-car market. [WSJ]

Single-dose detergent capsules were involved in roughly 10,000 cases of exposure involving young children in the past year, according to the American Association of Poison Control Centers. [WSJ]
