Were America’s millions of enslaved Africans property, or were they human beings with intrinsic rights? For John Marshall, the chief justice of the Supreme Court from 1801 to 1835, they were, astoundingly, both. Marshall conceded that slavery was “contrary to the law of nature,” since “every man has a natural right to the fruits of his own labor.” But for Marshall natural law did not trump human law. Slavery was condoned by custom and by the Constitution.
