Joseph Mitchell devotees—and we are many—do not tire of thinking or talking about the man, who was born in 1908, died in 1996 and wrote some of the greatest articles ever published in the New Yorker, or anywhere. We marvel at the architecture of his sentences. We stand in awe of the way he insinuated deep themes—mortality, despair, human folly—into what seem to start out as conventional magazine pieces about offbeat people, like the bearded lady at the circus, or colorful places, like McSorley’s Old Ale House.
