Late in 'Anna Karenina,' in some of the book's most luminous chapters, Tolstoy dramatizes the birth of Kitty and Levin's first child. The delivery is protracted and difficult; Kitty's suffering is acute. For Levin, too, the tension, the helplessness and anticipation are so intense that they seem to form a rift in his very understanding of life. He feels he has "lost sense of time." Despite years of atheism, he has begun involuntarily appealing to God and realizes that only once before, beside his brother's deathbed, had he experienced something comparable: "That had been grief and this was joy.
